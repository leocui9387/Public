INTRODUCTION - Investing for Anyone¶

This is a tutorial on the basic process of designing a market trading algorithm and testing it. The specific topic that is used to demostrate the design and testing of a market trading algorithm is the question of whether or not the Dollar Cost Averaging strategy can be beaten by Buy the Bottom Strategy when applied to investing in the S&P 500. The intention is to find a simple algorithm for anyone to use without any financial knowledge required. As such, the analytical tools that will be incorporated will be sparse, but the overall method can still be applied.

Dollar Cost Averaging(DCA) takes the idea of a monthly budget and applies it to investing in the stock market. Every month, a set amount of money is used to purchase shares of stocks. This idea came to prominence when John Bogle pioneered index funds and passive investments.

The Buy the Bottom(BtB) strategy was inspired by an interview with Mark Cuban where he proposed that investors should just save money and buy when there's a crash. This is significantly less stressful but also requires financial expertise. But the real question is, does BtB produce greater returns than DCA?

From an investor perspective, it is very difficult to beat the market. It requires having an advantage on some of the biggest corporations with some of the hardest working people in the USA. As such, the average retail investor will typically be unable to beat the market, so investing in the S&P 500 is the best default option. When there does exist some advantage, it is encumbant upon the investor to act by moving funds from the S&P 500 to the alternative with a higher perceived value in the future. This is the best strategy for everyday people and is what great investors advise.

However, armed with this knowledge, is there some algorithm that is better than a simple buy a fixed amount every month?

Basic Architecture¶

Most data sources have some sort of bandwidth limit, so the initial data load will download all the data and save it to a file. Afterwards, the file will be updated as time passes.

S & P 500¶

For S&P 500 data, Yahoo Finance was used as a source because it's free, open, and has daily OHLC(Open, High, Low, and Close) data. OHLC data is key for this analysis because we will be looking for market peaks and troughs using the Zig Zag indicator, which requires HLC data in order to be calculated. OHLC data is typically preferable to any single data point (i.e. Close) because it will reflect some of the tensions in the market. On a typical day on the stock market, the highest volume occurs during the end of the day followed by the start of the day. The High and Low will show the amount of price volatility in the day. Therefore, OHLC data is a good summary of what happened on the market that day and required for the Zig Zag indicator. There will be more discussion on the Zig Zag indicator later in the Exploratory Data Analysis.

The time period selected was Jan 1970 to now. The reason for this is because the stock market is generally managed via the Federal Funds Rate (FFR) in modern times. Using the FFR is nothing new, but Paul Volcker was the first to aggressively use it to fight inflation in the US during the 80s and establish the prescedent that the Fed manages the market via the FFR. Since then, the largest innovation or change to how the Fed manages the market is the use of Quantitative Easing(QE) created by Ben Bernanke after the 2009 mortgage crisis.

One possible motivation behind the creation of QE was the belief that taking the FFR to zero was not enough to stimulate the economy enough, and the fear that having a negative FFR would have severe and unpredictable effects, which is not good when the goal is to re-establish economic stability. However, using 2009 and the creation of QE as an analysis period would shrink an already small data set, which is stock market crash data. For this reason, January 1970 was a convenient limit to the Yahoo data set, although it is possible that the curators also had this in mind when specifiying the available dates.

Note:

A popular data set that is frequently used is the Robert Schiller data set. The problem with this data set is that it's monthly point data, and thus lacks much detail. Price volatility is important to analyzing markets and the lack of information on volatility is a severe weakness.

# Get Yahoo Data
def get_yahoo_sp500():
    
    def yahoo_time(in_time):
        return int(time.mktime(in_time.timetuple()))
    
    def downloader(ticker,start,end):
        interval = '1d' # 1d, 1m

        query_string = f'https://query1.finance.yahoo.com/v7/finance/download/{ticker}?' + \
                       f'period1={period1}&period2={period2}&interval={interval}' + \
                       f'&events=history&includeAdjustedClose=true'
        
        df = pd.read_csv(query_string)
        return df
    
    yahoo_file_name = "yahoo_sp500.csv"
    ticker = '^GSPC'
    
    if (not os.path.exists(yahoo_file_name)):
        # Download all data
        # Jan 1, 1970 corresponds to the zero value for the date time object used by Yahoo.
        period1 = yahoo_time(datetime.datetime(1970, 1, 1 , 23, 59))
        period2 = yahoo_time(datetime.datetime.now())

        raw_df = downloader(ticker,period1,period2)
        raw_df.to_csv(yahoo_file_name, index=False, header=True)
    else: 
        # Update Data
        raw_df = pd.read_csv(yahoo_file_name)
        last_date = datetime.datetime.strptime(raw_df["Date"].max(),"%Y-%m-%d")
        
        if datetime.datetime.now() > last_date:
            period1 = yahoo_time(last_date + datetime.timedelta(days = 1))
            period2 = yahoo_time(datetime.datetime.now())
            
            try:
                new_df = downloader(ticker,period1,period2)
                raw_df = pd.concat([raw_df,new_df])
                raw_df.to_csv(yahoo_file_name, index=False, header=True)
            except:
                print("limit exceeded")
            
    # Final Quality of Life Improvements
    raw_df = raw_df.rename(columns = {"Adj Close":"Adj_Close"})
    raw_df["Date"] = pd.to_datetime(raw_df["Date"])
    
    return raw_df

# CPI - two part function 
# 1) initial load that also saves the data to a file and return data
# 2) if file exists, then just update the file and return updated data

def CPIU_Getter( beg_year, end_year, force_download = False ):
    # function to get 10 years of CPI data
    def bls_looper (start_year):
        headers = {'Content-type': 'application/json'} 
        
        data = json.dumps({"seriesid": ["CUUR0000SA0"],
                           "startyear": str(start_year), 
                           "endyear": str(start_year + 10)})

        p = requests.post('https://api.bls.gov/publicAPI/v2/timeseries/data/', 
                          data=data, headers=headers)

        json_data = json.loads(p.text)['Results']['series'][0]["data"]
        tdf = pd.DataFrame(json_data)
        tdf["year"] = tdf["year"].astype(int)
        tdf["month"] = tdf["period"].apply(lambda x: int(x[1:]))

        return tdf

    cpi_file_name = "cpi_final_c4.csv"
    raw_drop_cols = ["periodName","period","footnotes"]
    
    # initial file load
    if (not os.path.exists(cpi_file_name) or force_download):
        # BLS only lets you download 10 years at a time, so looping is needed
        acc = []
        for n in range(beg_year, end_year + 10, 10):
            
            if n <= datetime.datetime.now().year : 
                tmp = bls_looper(n)
                acc.append(tmp)

        full = pd.concat(acc)
        full = full.drop(raw_drop_cols, axis = 1).sort_values(["year","month"])
        
        # saves the file to avoid bandwidth limit
        full.to_csv(cpi_file_name, index = False)
        dl_df = full
        
    else:
        # update CPI data file
        dl_df = pd.read_csv(cpi_file_name)
        max_yr = dl_df["year"].max()
        max_month = dl_df[dl_df["year"] == max_yr]["month"].max()
        
        if datetime.datetime.now() > datetime.datetime(max_yr, max_month,10):
            dl_yr = (max_yr if max_month != 12 else max_yr + 1)
            new_df = bls_looper(dl_yr)
            
            if max_month != 12:
                new_df = new_df[(new_df["year"] != max_yr) 
                                | ((new_df["year"] == max_yr) & (new_df["month"] > max_month))]
            
            dl_df = pd.concat([dl_df,new_df])
            raw_drop_cols.append("latest")
            dl_df = dl_df.drop(raw_drop_cols, axis = 1).sort_values(["year","month"])
            dl_df.to_csv(cpi_file_name, index=False, header=True)
            
    return dl_df

# small dataset with no bandwidth limit
def get_business_cycles(raw = False):
    
    start_date = "1969-7-1" # limited for Yahoo S&P data

    url = "http://data.nber.org/data/cycles/20210719_cycle_dates_pasted.csv"
    df = pd.read_csv(url)
    
    df["peak"] = pd.to_datetime(df["peak"])
    df["trough"] = pd.to_datetime(df["trough"])
    
    # limit data to relevant range
    df = df[df["peak"] > start_date]
    
    # transform data to match pivots format from Zig Zag algorithm
    if not raw :
        dfp = df[["peak"]].copy()
        dfp["Pivots"] = 1
        dfp["Date"] = dfp["peak"]

        dft = df[["trough"]].copy()
        dft["Pivots"] = -1
        dft["Date"] = dft["trough"]
        df = pd.concat([dfp, dft]).sort_values("Date").drop(["peak","trough"], axis = 1)
            
    return df

Zig Zag Indicator - TAKEN FROM THE INTERNET¶

The Zig Zag Indicator is a technical analysis tool that identifies peaks and troughs based on a percent change threshold. To remove the noise in the stock market, the Zig Zag indicator was used to filter out minor movements, so that we can analyze the major moves, which should correlate with the business cycle data from the NBER.

For the BtB strategy, the troughs will serve as a trigger to purchase stocks.

The Zig Zag algorithm is complex. An implementation was found from Stack Overflow and integrated into this workbook.

# From the Internet
PEAK, VALLEY = 1, -1

def _identify_initial_pivot(X, up_thresh, down_thresh):
# Quickly identify the X[0] as a peak or valley
    x_0 = X[0]
    max_x = x_0
    max_t = 0
    min_x = x_0
    min_t = 0
    up_thresh += 1
    down_thresh += 1

    for t in range(1, len(X)):
        x_t = X[t]

        if x_t / min_x >= up_thresh:
            return VALLEY if min_t == 0 else PEAK

        if x_t / max_x <= down_thresh:
            return PEAK if max_t == 0 else VALLEY

        if x_t > max_x:
            max_x = x_t
            max_t = t

        if x_t < min_x:
            min_x = x_t
            min_t = t

    t_n = len(X)-1
    
    return VALLEY if x_0 < X[t_n] else PEAK

# From the Internet
def peak_valley_pivots_candlestick(close, high, low, up_thresh, down_thresh):
    """
    Finds the peaks and valleys of a series of HLC (open is not necessary).
    TR: This is modified peak_valley_pivots function in order to find peaks and valleys for OHLC.
    Parameters
    ----------
    close : This is series with closes prices.
    high : This is series with highs  prices.
    low : This is series with lows prices.
    up_thresh : The minimum relative change necessary to define a peak.
    down_thesh : The minimum relative change necessary to define a valley.
    Returns
    -------
    an array with 0 indicating no pivot and -1 and 1 indicating valley and peak
    respectively
    Using Pandas
    ------------
    For the most part, close, high and low may be a pandas series. However, the index must
    either be [0,n) or a DateTimeIndex. Why? This function does X[t] to access
    each element where t is in [0,n).
    The First and Last Elements
    ---------------------------
    The first and last elements are guaranteed to be annotated as peak or
    valley even if the segments formed do not have the necessary relative
    changes. This is a tradeoff between technical correctness and the
    propensity to make mistakes in data analysis. The possible mistake is
    ignoring data outside the fully realized segments, which may bias analysis.
    """
    if down_thresh > 0:
        raise ValueError('The down_thresh must be negative.')

    initial_pivot = _identify_initial_pivot(close, up_thresh, down_thresh)

    t_n = len(close)
    pivots = np.zeros(t_n, dtype='i1')
    pivots[0] = initial_pivot

    # Adding one to the relative change thresholds saves operations. Instead
    # of computing relative change at each point as x_j / x_i - 1, it is
    # computed as x_j / x_1. Then, this value is compared to the threshold + 1.
    # This saves (t_n - 1) subtractions.
    up_thresh += 1
    down_thresh += 1

    trend = -initial_pivot
    last_pivot_t = 0
    last_pivot_x = close[0]
    for t in range(1, len(close)):

        if trend == -1:
            x = low[t]
            r = x / last_pivot_x
            if r >= up_thresh:
                pivots[last_pivot_t] = trend#
                trend = 1
                #last_pivot_x = x
                last_pivot_x = high[t]
                last_pivot_t = t
            elif x < last_pivot_x:
                last_pivot_x = x
                last_pivot_t = t
        else:
            x = high[t]
            r = x / last_pivot_x
            if r <= down_thresh:
                pivots[last_pivot_t] = trend
                trend = -1
                #last_pivot_x = x
                last_pivot_x = low[t]
                last_pivot_t = t
            elif x > last_pivot_x:
                last_pivot_x = x
                last_pivot_t = t


    if last_pivot_t == t_n-1:
        pivots[last_pivot_t] = trend
    elif pivots[t_n-1] == 0:
        pivots[t_n-1] = trend

    return pivots

# Created by Leo to get pivot data
def get_zig_zag_pivots(df, threshold_pct):
    
    def thingy(reau):
        if reau["Pivots"] == 1:
            return reau["High"]
        elif reau["Pivots"] == -1:
            return reau["Low"]
        else:
            return np.NAN
    
    print("Calculating Pivots for ", threshold_pct * 100, "%")
    # data parse
    df_out = df.copy()
    down_threshold = -threshold_pct
    
    pivots = peak_valley_pivots_candlestick(df.Close.values, df.High.values, df.Low.values ,threshold_pct,down_threshold)
    
    df_out['Pivots'] = pivots
    df_out['Pivot_Price'] = np.nan  # This line clears old pivot prices
    
    df_out['Pivot_Price'] = df_out.apply(thingy, axis = 1)
        
    return df_out

Reviewing the Plot¶

What we see here is a few of the quirks of stock market data. There seems to be some sort of log normal distribution of the pivot points. This makes sense because returns can not occur over negative time, the most you can lose is 100%, and there's no limit to how long a bull market runs or the returns of the bull market.

There appears to be 4 overall clusters, but if that is further split between bear and bull markets, then we see 3 clusters of bull markets and 3 clusters of bear markets. Unfortunately, as with most analysis of major events, there's no statistically representative sample and even between events, the world changes so much that it might not be relevant to compare across time periods.

Overall, it looks like the median bear markest last for about 1.4 years and the median bull markets last for about 5.1 years.

Hypothesis Testing¶

  • Null Hypothesis: returns from BtB are not significantly different from DCA returns
  • Alternative Hypothesis: returns from BtB is significantly different from DCA returns

To test this we will need to create a back testing system. The back testing system will take buy/sell rules and calculate the returns. A typical back testing algorithm will typically find buy/sell triggers and apply those triggers to a particular stock without regard to funding because funding is managed by another risk management algorithm.

For the purpose of modeling the investments of an individual, we will build a back testing system that will also have a funding calculation mechanism. For the purposes of this analysis, the funding algorithm will be a fixed value with CPI adjustment. Without a CPI adjustment, there will be a natural favor toward DCA because the earliest possible investment is typically the best. Earlier investments are better because there's more time for the investment to accrue and the stock market has always had higher highs and higher lows.

def backtester(price_df, fn_funding, fn_purchases):

def dca_funding(days_between_purchases = 30, init_day_offset = 0, base_amount = 100, cpi = None):
    def closure(df):
        
        # get date range
        start = df["Date"].min() + datetime.timedelta(days = init_day_offset)
        end = df["Date"].max()

        out_list = []
        date = start
        
        # create days between start and end in even periods
        while date < end:
            out_list.append(date)
            date = date + datetime.timedelta(days = days_between_purchases)
        
        datez = pd.DataFrame(data= {"Date1":out_list})
        tmp_df = pd.DataFrame({"Date1":df["Date"].copy(), "Date": df["Date"].copy()})
        df1 = pd.merge_asof(datez, tmp_df, on="Date1", direction = "nearest")
        df1 = df1.drop(["Date1"],axis = 1)
        
        # add columns for quality of life improvement for analysis
        df1["year"] = df1["Date"].apply(lambda x : x.year)
        df1["month"] = df1["Date"].apply(lambda x : x.month)
        
        # allows for adjustment due to CPI
        # can be used to create functional funding strategies
        if cpi is None:
            df3["funding"] = base_amount
            
        else:
            base_index = cpi[(cpi["year"] == start.year) & (cpi["month"] == start.month)]["value"].values[0]

            adj_cpi = cpi.copy()
            adj_cpi["adj_index"] = adj_cpi["value"].apply(lambda x: float(x)/base_index)

            df3 = df1.merge(adj_cpi, left_on=["year","month"],right_on=["year","month"] ,how='left')

            df3["real_funding"] = base_amount
            df3["funding"] = df3["adj_index"] * df3["real_funding"]
            
            df3["funding"] = df3["funding"].apply(lambda x: round(x,2))
            df3 = df3.drop(["real_funding", "adj_index", "year","month", "value"], axis = 1)
        
        # final clean up
        df = df.merge(df3, left_on="Date", right_on="Date", how="left") 
        
        df["funding"] = df["funding"].fillna(0)
        df["total_invested"] = df["funding"].cumsum()
        
        return df
            
            
    return closure

Purchasing Function¶

def dca_strategy(df):
    # calculate purchases (changes within a period)
    df["purchase_price"] = (df["High"] + df["Low"] + 2 * df["Close"])/4
    df["purchased_shares"] = df["funding"]/df["purchase_price"]
    df["purchased_shares"] = df["purchased_shares"].apply(lambda x: 0 if (x == float("inf")) else x)
    
    # calculate balances (aggregation of changes)
    df["total_shares"] = df["purchased_shares"].cumsum()
    df["total_value"] = df["total_shares"] * df["Close"]
    df["total_value"] = df["total_value"].apply(lambda x: round(x,2))
    
    df["pct_return"] = ((df["total_value"]/df["total_invested"]) - 1) * 100   
    
    return df

Strategy - Buy the Bottom Strategy¶

For this strategy, I'm going to initially give the strategy an advantage by having it buy at the lowest price during the business cycle. If there's a significant advantage, then further investigation will be warranted. Knowing the peaks and troughs is a significant advantage that can't be used in a real strategy because no one knows exactly when tops and bottoms will happen. However, this simplifies the programming so we can check to see if there's some value worth further investigation.

def pure_bottom_purchase_strategy(df, threshold):
    
    pivots = get_zig_zag_pivots(df, threshold).dropna()
    
    def closure (df):
        
        # get the troughs
        trough_datez = pivots[pivots["Pivots"] == -1]["Date"].values
        df["purchase_price"] = (df["High"] + df["Low"] + 2 * df["Close"])/4
        
        # calculate purchases with troughs as the trigger to move
        # all savings into stocks
        savings_balance = 0
        for i, r in df.iterrows():
            savings_balance = savings_balance + r["funding"]
            if r["Date"] in trough_datez:
                df.loc[i,"purchased_shares"] = savings_balance/r["purchase_price"]
                savings_balance = 0
            
            df.loc[i,"savings_balance"] = savings_balance
        
        # calculation of balances
        df["total_shares"] = df["purchased_shares"].cumsum()
        df["total_shares"] = df["total_shares"].fillna(method="ffill")
        df["total_value"] = df["total_shares"] * df["Close"] +  df["savings_balance"]
        df["total_value"] = df["total_value"].apply(lambda x: round(x,2))
        df["pct_return"] = ((df["total_value"]/df["total_invested"]) - 1) * 100   
        
        return df
        
    return closure

Buy the Bottom Strategy - Review¶

Buy the Bottom is significantly different from Dollar Cost Averaging. However, the difference is a significant disadvantage. So even if you knew exactly when to buy at the bottom, you won't be able to beat DCA in the past 50 years. This is likely due to all the opportunities to buy at a price lower than the next trough being missed with the BtB strategy.

The major strength of buying the bottom is the lack of stress associated with holding during the falling phase of the market. However, this comes at the expense of missing out on all the value that could have been generated by shares purchased at a price lower than the next trough.

Another major issue is the fact that you can't possibly expect anyone to know the lowest point of the trough without hindsight. With the knowledge of the average length of a bear and bull market, and knowledge of what percentage drop constitutes a "crash", is it possible to combine these to create a more realistic algorithm that doesn't require hindsight?

Strategy - Check Mark Strategy¶

The idea behind the Check Mark strategy is at some drop threshold, we will start buying using the savings that have been accrued since we stopped buying once a bull market is past the average bull market.

This will be more realistic than BtB because the strategy does not require the hindsight of knowing the troughs and allow for the continued purchase of shares for sometime after a trough.

def check_mark_purchase_plan(df, pivots, purchase_threshold, savings_periods, dca_periods, period_length):
    pivots["trigger_price"] = pivots["Pivot_Price"] * (1 - purchase_threshold)
    pivots["Date_+1"] = pivots["Date"].shift(periods=-1)
    
    
    df["purchase_price"] = (df["High"] + df["Low"] + 2 * df["Close"])/4
    purchase_plans = []
    
    prior_purchase_start_date = np.datetime64('1941-12-07') 
    for i,r in pivots.iterrows():
        # prevents overlapping plans 
        bear_start_date = r["Date"]
        bear_end_date = r["Date_+1"]

        tmp_df = df[(df['Date'] >= bear_start_date) & (df['Date'] <= bear_end_date)]
        tmp_df = tmp_df[tmp_df["purchase_price"] < r["trigger_price"]].sort_values("Date")

        if len(tmp_df.index) > 0:
            prior_purchase_end_date = prior_purchase_start_date + \
                np.timedelta64(int(savings_periods + dca_periods)*period_length, 'D')

            if prior_purchase_end_date > tmp_df["Date"].values[0]:
                continue

            purchase_start_date = tmp_df["Date"].values[0]
            prior_purchase_start_date = purchase_start_date
            i = 0

            one_p_plan = []
            date = purchase_start_date
            
            # generate purchase plan
            while i < (savings_periods + dca_periods):
                i = i + 1
                one_p_plan.append(date)
                date = date + np.timedelta64(period_length, 'D')

            datez_df = pd.DataFrame({"purchase_plan_date":one_p_plan})
            datez_df["purchase_phase"] = datez_df.index.map(
                lambda x: "savings" if x < savings_periods else "dca")
            purchase_plans.append(datez_df)
            
    return purchase_plans

def check_mark_strategy(drop_threshold, purchase_threshold, 
                        savings_periods, dca_periods, period_length = 30):
    
    pivots = get_zig_zag_pivots(df, drop_threshold).dropna()
    
    
    def closure (df):
        # create purchase plans
        purchase_plans = check_mark_purchase_plan(df,pivots,purchase_threshold,savings_periods, dca_periods, period_length)
        final_plan = pd.concat(purchase_plans).sort_values("purchase_plan_date")
        df_planz = pd.merge_asof(right=df["Date"], left=final_plan, right_on="Date", left_on = "purchase_plan_date",direction='nearest')
        
        # prevent purchase after last date
        df_planz= df_planz[df_planz["purchase_plan_date"] < df["Date"].iloc[-1]] 
        
        # apply the plans
        df = df.merge(df_planz, how="left", on= "Date")
        
        savings_counter = 0
        savings_balance = 0
        for i,r in df.iterrows():
            savings_balance = savings_balance + r["funding"]
            
            if not pd.isna(r["purchase_phase"]):
                # use savings
                if r["purchase_phase"] == "savings":
                    if savings_counter == 0 :
                        savings_counter = savings_periods
                    
                    purchase_amount = savings_balance / savings_counter
                    shares_purchased = purchase_amount/r["purchase_price"]
                    df.loc[i,"purchased_shares"] = shares_purchased
                    savings_balance = savings_balance - purchase_amount
                    savings_counter = savings_counter - 1
                else:
                    if savings_counter == 0 :
                        savings_counter = dca_periods
                    
                    purchase_amount = savings_balance
                    shares_purchased = purchase_amount/r["purchase_price"]
                    df.loc[i,"purchased_shares"] = shares_purchased
                    savings_balance = savings_balance - purchase_amount
                    savings_counter = savings_counter - 1
                    
            df.loc[i,"savings_balance"] = savings_balance
        
        df["total_shares"] = df["purchased_shares"].cumsum()        
        df["total_value"] = df["total_shares"] * df["Close"] +  df["savings_balance"]
        return df
        
    return closure

Check Mark Strategy - Review¶

The pattern looks like a BtB with a trail afterwards. Although there's a chance that there's significant overfitting of the drop threshold, the relevant data set is too limited to do much more and 27% does seem to generalize from pre-90s to post-90s crashes. The 90s were the only time 27% failed, however, that was a unique situation that savy investors would have recognized. Therefore, I would conclude that 27% is actually a very important market indicator for the S&P500.

This implementation of the Check Mark strategy, apppears to have two major issues: prolonged bear markets and prolonged bull markets. Check Mark avoids a significant amount of the gains from the 90s bull market and also gets back in afterward, in the prolonged bear market of the early 2000s. After the real estate crash of 2009, the strategy also avoids all the gains from the bull market of the 2010s.

Of the two issues, avoiding the prolonged bull markets is the most damning. The 90s were a unique time and can be ignored as an outlier, but the post 2009 boom shows the that prolonged bull markets are a real thing, which may be caused by better economic policy(another source of endogeneity). As the graph is on a logarithmic scale, significant increases are actually exponential increases, and prolonged bull markets are where the market moves into another "order of magnitude". The logarithmic scale can be removed to view the full extent of the missed gains.

In a bid to avoid bear markets, which the 27% threshold seems to does well in identifying, we cut short the major bull runs, which causes the Check Mark strategy to lag behind DCA even worse than BtB.

CONCLUSION¶

Based on this analysis, DCA is the best strategy amongst the ones tested. Although a better algorithm was not discovered, some important lessons can be learned by reviewing the weaknesses of the alternative strategies (see Check Mark Strategy Review for more).

However, for the average reader who doesn't want to spend too much time learning about the financial markets, stick with Dollar Cost Averaging. No one has found a significantly better algorithm. Value Averaging is a promising alternative, but due to time constraints, this was not modeled. The link below demonstrates the Value Averaging algorithm, but doesn't test it over a very long time period.

The biggest piece of wisdom to add is "don't try to catch a falling knife". This is a saying from investors warning against picking the bottom. This is a knife catching/market timing strategy and this might be evidence that you should avoid trying to time the market.

Further Investigation¶

Most investors believe that once the Fed raises rates, the party is over, which is why we see things like taper tandrum or the early 2022 drop. However, this is not easily modeled because sometimes the market ignores the Fed as in the 1990s and 2000s. With the small dataset of crashes available and the inherent unpredictability of future systemic issues, creating a FFR based algorithm is difficult and couldn't be done due to time constraints.

The benefit is that you can use the FFR as a proxy for expert opinion on the state of the economy and also when Wall Street is getting worried about their greed getting the best of them. This is a reasonable statement because the Fed is influenced by the major players on Wall Street so it's a way for the investor to use someone else's expertise to their advantage.
